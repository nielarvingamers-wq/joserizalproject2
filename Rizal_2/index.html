Welcome to Rizal Website

National Hero of the Philippines | Physician | Novelist

The Pride of the Malayan Race

Dr. Jose Rizal (1861–1896) was a Filipino polymath whose life and works were instrumental in the Philippine struggle for independence. As a brilliant scholar who mastered over 22 languages, he used knowledge and literature rather than violence to challenge colonial rule.

His two most famous novels, Noli Me Tángere and El Filibusterismo, exposed the corruption of the colonial era and unified the Filipino spirit. Today, he remains a symbol of peaceful resistance and intellectual excellence.

Visual Representation of Dr. Jose Rizal

José Rizal

The Great Malayan • 1861–1896

Architect of Liberty

José Protasio Rizal was a man of peace who used his intellect to challenge an empire. As a physician and a scholar, he understood that the "social cancer" affecting the Philippines could only be cured by exposing the truth through education and literature.

His travels across Europe allowed him to gather support for the Propaganda Movement. By blending his medical expertise with a sharp literary mind, he became a voice for the voiceless, demanding dignity and legal equality for all Filipinos.

"I wish to show those who deny us Patriotism that we know how to die for our duty and our convictions."

A Legacy in Ink

His novels, Noli Me Tángere and El Filibusterismo, remain the foundation of Filipino literature. These works did not just tell stories; they acted as a mirror, forcing the colonial society to see its own corruption. His ultimate sacrifice at Bagumbayan turned a writer into a martyr and a martyr into a symbol of a nation's birth.

The Rizal Ancestral House in Calamba, Laguna.
The Making of a Genius

The Early Years of Pepe

Born on the moonlight night of Wednesday, June 19, 1861, in the lakeside town of Calamba, José Rizal was the seventh of eleven children. His mother, Teodora Alonso, almost lost her life during his delivery because of his unusually large head—an early physical sign of the giant intellect he would eventually possess.

The Influence of Doña Teodora

His first teacher was his mother. At the age of three, he learned the alphabet and the prayers from her. She taught him the value of discipline and the beauty of literature through the famous story of the "Moth and the Flame," which left a lasting impression on young José about the cost of seeking the light of truth.

"My mother is a woman of more than average education... She knows literature and speaks Spanish better than I do. She corrected my poems and gave me good advice when I was studying rhetoric." — Jose Rizal
Early Signs of Artistry

Even as a child known by the nickname "Pepe," Rizal showed remarkable talents that amazed his family and townspeople:

  • Age 5: He began to sketch and mold clay/wax figures of animals and people.
  • Age 8: He wrote his first poem in the native language, "Sa Aking Mga Kabata" (To My Fellow Youth), which championed the love for one's own language.
  • Religious Devotion: Young José was a regular at the town church and was often jokingly called "Manong José" because of his piety.

Despite being small and somewhat frail in stature, he spent his afternoons walking by the shores of Laguna de Bay with his dog, Usman, wondering what lay across the waters and dreaming of a better future for his people.

Lifelong Education

The Intellectual Evolution of José Rizal (1861 – 1896)

The First Teacher

Calamba & Biñan (1864 – 1871)

Rizal’s education began at home with his mother, Teodora Alonso, who taught him the alphabet and prayers. At age 9, he was sent to Biñan to study under Maestro Justiniano Aquino Cruz, where he quickly exhausted the local curriculum, mastering Latin and Spanish faster than any other student.

First Lessons in Latin & Spanish

The Jesuit Discipline

Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872 – 1877)

Entering as a teenager, Rizal excelled in the rigid Jesuit system. He was a consistent "Emperor" of his class and graduated at 16 with a Bachelor of Arts degree. He also earned a vocational title as a Perito Agrimensor (Expert Surveyor) during this time.

Graduated Summa Cum Laude (Sobresaliente)

Philosophy & Medicine

University of Santo Tomas (1877 – 1882)

He balanced studies in Philosophy and Letters with a specialized track in Medicine. It was here that his mother's failing eyesight led him to choose Ophthalmology as his life's vocation.

Shift to Medical Sciences

The Global Scholar

Spain, France, & Germany (1882 – 1887)

In Europe, he completed his Licentiate in Medicine at the Universidad Central de Madrid. He then traveled to Paris and Heidelberg to train under the world's leading eye surgeons, becoming one of the most skilled ophthalmologists of the 19th century.

Doctor of Medicine | Master of 22 Languages

The Practical Educator

Exile in Dapitan (1892 – 1896)

Even in exile, his "education" continued. He conducted scientific research in biology and engineering and founded a school for local boys, teaching them languages, mathematics, and martial arts, embodying his belief that "knowledge is the path to freedom."

Scientist, Engineer, and Mentor
"Without education and liberty, that soil and that sun of mankind, no reform is possible."
— José Rizal

The Mind of a Reformer

"I have always loved my poor country, and I am sure that I shall love her until my last moment... I shall die praying for her."

Education as Liberation

Rizal believed that the "Social Cancer" of the Philippines could only be cured through Education. He argued that a people cannot be free until they are enlightened and capable of self-governance.

Non-Violent Reform

Unlike the Katipunan's initial push for armed revolt, Rizal’s philosophy focused on Assimilation and Representation. He believed in winning freedom through the pen, the law, and moral integrity.

Racial Equality

Through his scientific and historical research, Rizal dismantled the "Indio" stereotype. He proved that Filipinos were naturally capable and that their "indolence" was a result of colonial oppression, not biology.

The "New Filipino"

He envisioned a New Society where Filipinos were not just subjects of Spain, but citizens of the world—disciplined, hardworking, and deeply rooted in their own culture and language.

Religious Reason

Rizal was not anti-religion, but anti-fanaticism. He advocated for a Faith guided by Reason, criticizing the friars for using religion to keep the masses in ignorance and fear.

Nationalism

His philosophy of Love of Country was not based on hatred of foreigners, but on the duty of every Filipino to improve themselves for the sake of the motherland.

The Grand Tour of a Reformer

The First Departure (1882)

Leaving the Philippines secretly to avoid Spanish suspicion, Rizal traveled via the Suez Canal to Spain. In Barcelona, he wrote "Amor Patrio" (Love of Country), his first nationalistic essay on foreign soil. He later moved to Madrid to complete his licentiate in Medicine and Philosophy, immersing himself in the liberal atmosphere of the Spanish capital.

The Scientific Specialization (1885-1887)

Rizal moved to Paris to work as an assistant to Dr. Louis de Wecker, a world-renowned ophthalmologist. He then headed to Heidelberg, Germany, where he famously wrote the poem "A las Flores de Heidelberg." In Berlin, he befriended great scientists like Dr. Rudolf Virchow and finally published 2,000 copies of Noli Me Tángere with the financial help of Maximo Viola.

The 1888 World Tour

After a brief and dangerous return to the Philippines, Rizal left again. He visited Hong Kong and Macau, then spent a month in Japan, where he studied Japanese art, music, and the Ju-jitsu style of self-defense. He then crossed the Pacific to San Francisco and traveled across the entire United States by train, arriving in New York before sailing to London.

The London Annotation (1888-1889)

In London, Rizal stayed with the Beckett family and spent his days at the British Museum. He manually copied and annotated Antonio de Morga's Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas. This was his most important historical work, as it proved to the world that Filipinos had a flourishing civilization before the Spanish arrived.

Vessel & Voyage Facts
  • Ship SS Salvadora: The ship that first carried him away from Manila in 1882.
  • Ship SS Belgic: The steamer he took from Japan to San Francisco in 1888.
  • Fact USA Observations: While he loved American progress, he wrote that it was "the land par excellence of freedom but only for the whites."
  • Fact Belgium: In 1891, he stayed in Ghent to print his second novel, El Filibusterismo, while living in extreme poverty.

Jose Rizal: The Romantic Hero

Exploring the hearts he touched across the globe.

While Dr. Jose Rizal is celebrated for his patriotism, his personal life was a tapestry of deep affections and bittersweet partings. Here are the women who played a role in the life of the Great Malayan.

The Puppy Love

Segunda Katigbak

Met when Rizal was only 16, Segunda was his "first love." However, she was already engaged to Manuel Luz. Their relationship ended with a quiet, painful goodbye as Rizal was too shy to profess his feelings directly.

The Girl Next Door

Leonor Valenzuela (Orang)

His neighbor in Intramuros. Rizal courted her using "invisible ink" (saltwater and lamp warmth). It was a lighthearted romance that faded when he left for Spain.

The One True Love

Leonor Rivera

His cousin and sweetheart for 11 years. She is the inspiration for Maria Clara in his novels. Their love was thwarted by her mother and distance, eventually ending in her tragic marriage to Henry Kipping.

The Madrid Memory

Consuelo Ortiga y Rey

A beautiful woman in Madrid to whom Rizal dedicated a poem. He backed out of the relationship to remain loyal to Leonor Rivera and to avoid conflict with his friend Eduardo de Lete.

The Woman of Japan

Seiko Usui (O-Sei-San)

A Japanese samurai's daughter who taught Rizal painting and Japanese language. He almost stayed in Japan for her, but his patriotic mission called him back to Europe.

The London Companion

Gertrude Beckett

The daughter of his landlord in London. She fell deeply in love with him, but Rizal eventually left for Paris to avoid leading her on, as he could not reciprocate her feelings.

The Almost Engagement

Nellie Boustead

An intelligent and athletic woman. Rizal proposed marriage, but it failed because he refused to convert to Protestantism and her mother disapproved of him.

The Belgian Romance

Suzanne Jacoby

They met while Rizal was staying at a boarding house in Brussels. She was heartbroken when he left for Madrid, writing him letters of longing.

The Final Partner

Josephine Bracken

An Irish woman who met Rizal in Dapitan. She became his common-law wife and stayed by his side until his execution in 1896. She is famously called his "dulce extranjera."

The Rizal Art Gallery

A Showcase of the Hero's Visual Masterpieces

Dr. Jose Rizal was not just a man of words, but a man of visual precision. From his childhood in Calamba to his exile in Dapitan, he used his hands to carve, sketch, and paint the soul of his nation.

Sculptures

The Triumph of Science over Death

Created during his stay in Brussels, this clay sculpture depicts a young, naked woman standing upon a skull while holding a torch, symbolizing human enlightenment conquering mortality.

The Mother's Revenge

Made in Dapitan, this allegorical piece shows a mother dog attacking a crocodile. It is widely interpreted as a symbol of the Philippines' struggle against colonial oppression.

Sketches & Drawings

Self-Portrait (1887)

Rizal was a master of the pencil. This self-portrait, drawn during his time in Germany, shows his meticulous attention to detail and his deep, contemplative gaze.

Leonor Rivera

Rizal often sketched the people he loved. This sketch of Leonor Rivera remains one of the most iconic images of his "true love," capturing her grace and melancholy.

Paintings

Portrait of Saturnina

One of the few surviving oil paintings by Rizal. It shows his eldest sister, Saturnina, and demonstrates his skill in handling color and light in the European classical style.

Travel Sketches & Watercolors

Rizal filled his diaries with watercolors of the landscapes he saw in Spain, France, and Germany, serving as a visual record of his travels and botanical observations.

"To the Filipino people, I leave the work of my hands as a testament to the beauty of our race."

The Poetic Works of Dr. Jose Rizal

A comprehensive collection of the National Hero's literary contributions.

Written: December 1896 | Language: Spanish

Mi Último Adiós (My Last Farewell)

Rizal's final literary gift to his country, written in his cell at Fort Santiago before his execution. He didn't give it a title; his friends later named it. It serves as a stirring goodbye to his "Patria Adorada" (Adored Fatherland).

Significance: It redefined Filipino patriotism, showing that one can serve the country through the ultimate sacrifice of life.
Written: 1879 | Language: Spanish

A La Juventud Filipina (To the Philippine Youth)

Written when he was a student at UST, this poem won first prize in a literary contest. It was groundbreaking because it openly referred to the Philippines as the "Fatherland" (Patria) of the Filipinos, rather than Spain.

Significance: It contains the famous line calling the youth the "Fair hope of my Fatherland," urging them to seek excellence in arts and sciences.
Written: 1869 (at age 8) | Language: Tagalog

Sa Aking Mga Kabatà (To My Fellow Youth)

Commonly attributed to an 8-year-old Rizal, this poem emphasizes the love for one's native language. It argues that language is a gift from God and a symbol of liberty.

Significance: It establishes the idea that national identity is tied to linguistic pride: "He who does not love his own language is worse than a beast and a stinking fish."
Written: 1886 | Language: Spanish

A Las Flores de Heidelberg (To the Flowers of Heidelberg)

Written during his time in Germany. Overwhelmed by homesickness while walking along the Neckar River, Rizal wrote to the flowers, asking them to carry his message of faith and love back to the Philippines.

Significance: A poignant look at Rizal's vulnerability and his constant longing for his home despite the beauty of Europe.
Written: 1895 | Language: Spanish

Mi Retiro (My Retreat)

Rizal wrote this during his exile in Dapitan. It is a long, descriptive poem about his simple, lonely, but peaceful life by the sea, written at the request of his mother.

Significance: Shows Rizal’s resilience and his ability to find beauty and intellectual stimulation even while being a political prisoner.

Noli Me Tangere

The "Social Cancer" that Awakened a Nation

Published in Berlin in 1887, this novel was Rizal’s diagnosis of the Philippine society under Spanish colonial rule. It exposed the corruption of the clergy and the government, sparking the flames of the Philippine Revolution.

The History of the Book

Rizal began writing the novel in Madrid, continued it in Paris, and finished the final chapters in Germany. He was nearly destitute and almost burned the manuscript until his friend, Maximo Viola, loaned him the money for printing 2,000 copies.

The Meaning: The title is Latin for "Touch Me Not," a reference to John 20:17. Rizal used this to describe a "cancer" so terrible that the slightest touch causes excruciating pain to the patient (the Philippines).

The Symbolic Characters

  • Juan Crisostomo Ibarra: A reform-minded Filipino who returned from Europe. He represents Rizal's own idealistic side.
  • Maria Clara: The epitome of Filipino womanhood, representing the oppressed but graceful Motherland.
  • Padre Damaso: The arrogant Franciscan friar who represents the abusive power of the Spanish clergy.
  • Elias: The mysterious pilot and outlaw who represents the common people (Filipino masses) ready for revolution.
  • Sisa: A mother driven to insanity, symbolizing the tragic state of the suffering Philippines.

The Storyline

The story follows Ibarra's return to San Diego to build a school. However, he discovers the truth behind his father's death—persecuted by the friars and denied a Christian burial. His efforts for reform are met with a staged rebellion intended to frame him, leading to his imprisonment and the tragic loss of Maria Clara to a nunnery.

The novel ends with the death of Elias, who sacrifices himself so that Ibarra (representing the hope of the nation) can escape to fight another day.

“I have unmasked the hypocrisy that under the guide of religion came to impoverish and to brutalize us.” — Jose Rizal

El Filibusterismo

The Reign of Greed | The Sequel of Revolution

Published in Ghent, Belgium in 1891, El Filibusterismo is the more political, darker, and more cynical sequel to Noli Me Tangere. It shifts from the hope of reform to the inevitability of revolution.

The Protagonist

From Ibarra to Simoun

Thirteen years after fleeing the Philippines, Crisostomo Ibarra returns as Simoun, a wealthy and influential jeweler. No longer an idealist, he has become a "dark cardinal" who uses his wealth to corrupt Spanish officials, hoping to provoke a revolution by making the people's suffering unbearable.

"Simoun does not want reform; he wants to destroy the system from within."

The Climax

The Pomegranate Lamp

The novel centers on Simoun’s failed attempt to blow up a wedding feast attended by the Governor-General and the friars. He creates a nitro-glycerin bomb hidden inside an ornate lamp. The plan fails when Isagani, out of love for the bride, throws the lamp into the river, inadvertently saving the colonizers.

Tone:Political, Bitter, Revenge-driven
Key Conflict:Violent Revolution vs. Peaceful Reform
Ending:The death of Simoun and the disposal of his treasure into the sea.
The Inspiration

Dedicated to GOMBURZA

Rizal dedicated this second novel to the memory of the three priests—Mariano Gomez, Jose Burgos, and Jacinto Zamora—who were executed in 1872. This event was the catalyst for Rizal's own political awakening and the "filibuster" (subversive) sentiment in the Philippines.

“Where are the youth who will consecrate their golden hours, their illusions, and their enthusiasm to the welfare of their native land?” — Padre Florentino, El Filibusterismo

The Mercado-Rizal Clan

The Foundation of a Hero: The Family of Calamba

Jose Rizal was born into a wealthy and educated family in Calamba, Laguna. He was the seventh of eleven children born to Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso.

The Father

Francisco Mercado Rizal

A hardworking and independent-minded farmer from Biñan, Jose called him a "model of fathers." He leased lands from the Dominican estate in Calamba and was highly respected in the community. It was he who instilled in Jose the value of discipline and work ethic.

The Mother

Teodora Alonso Realonda

Jose’s first teacher and his greatest influence. A woman of remarkable intelligence and refined culture, she was the one who taught Jose his alphabet and prayers. Her unjust imprisonment by the Spanish authorities was one of the primary reasons Jose pursued medicine (ophthalmology) to cure her failing eyesight.

The Second Father

Paciano Rizal

Jose’s only brother and his "mentor." Paciano was ten years older and served as a bridge between Jose and the revolutionary movement. He supported Jose’s studies in Europe financially and emotionally, acting as a second father to him throughout his life.

The 11 Siblings

The Rizal Sisters

Jose grew up surrounded by nine sisters. Their bonds were close, and they were often his confidants during his exile and final days.

1. Saturnina: The eldest; helped fund Jose's education.
2. Narcisa: The most hospitable; could recite all of Jose's poems.
3. Olimpia: The sister who acted as the middleman between Jose and Segunda Katigbak.
4. Lucia: Her husband was denied a Christian burial by friars, fueling Jose's anger.
5. Maria: The sister Jose confided in about his plans to marry Josephine Bracken.
6. Concepcion: "Concha," who died at age 3. Her death was Jose's first sorrow.
7. Josefa: Joined the Katipunan and became one of its first female members.
8. Trinidad: The one to whom Jose gave the alcohol lamp containing 'Mi Ultimo Adios'.
9. Soledad: The youngest sibling; she was a teacher and the best-educated of the sisters.

“I had a father, a mother, brothers and sisters; I was loved. Now I go to a foreign land, but not really foreign, for I feel I am at home everywhere.” — Jose Rizal

The Life and Martyrdom of Jose Rizal

A Chronological Journey from Calamba to Bagumbayan

1861 – 1870

Birth and Early Childhood

The Seventh Child of Calamba

Jose Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna. He was a frail child with an unusually large head. His mother, Teodora, was his first teacher, telling him stories like the "Parable of the Moth" which foreshadowed his own fate of seeking the light of truth at the cost of his life.

1872 – 1882

The Years of Learning

The Jesuit and Dominican Education

Rizal entered Ateneo Municipal de Manila, where he excelled in every subject and graduated with highest honors. In 1872, the execution of the GOMBURZA priests deeply affected his family. He later studied Medicine at the University of Santo Tomas before deciding to finish his studies in Spain to escape the stifling atmosphere of the colony.

1882 – 1892

The European Odyssey

Pen as the Sword

In Europe, Rizal joined the Propaganda Movement, writing for La Solidaridad. He mastered multiple languages and completed his two greatest novels: Noli Me Tangere (1887) and El Filibusterismo (1891). These books exposed the "social cancer" of the Philippines, making him the primary enemy of the Spanish friars.

1892 – 1896

The Exile

The Enlightened Prisoner

Upon his return to the Philippines, Rizal founded La Liga Filipina. He was quickly arrested and deported to Dapitan. During his four-year exile, he lived a productive life: practicing medicine, building a school for boys, improving the town’s water system, and discovering new species of animals.

1896

The Final Sacrifice

Consummatum Est

After being implicated in the Katipunan revolution (despite his lack of direct involvement), Rizal was tried by a military court for rebellion. On the morning of December 30, 1896, at Bagumbayan (now Rizal Park), he was executed by firing squad. His last words were "Consummatum est!" (It is finished).

"I die without seeing the dawn brighten over my native land. You who have it to see, welcome it—and forget not those who have fallen during the night." — Jose Rizal
